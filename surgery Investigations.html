üîÑ Intestinal Obstruction

  • Plain X-ray abdomen erect position
    Shows dilated small bowel loops with air-fluid levels. Jejunum shows circular folds ("valvulae connivents"), ileum shows shapeless tubes, and the colon shows typical haustrations.
  • US (Ultrasound)
    Can detect distention and mass.
  • CT (Computed Tomography)
    May show a transition zone and the cause of obstruction, though its sensitivity is less than 50%.
  • Barium enema
    Used when colonic obstruction is suspected.
  • Laboratory investigations
    TLC (Total Leukocyte Count): Markedly rises in cases of strangulation.
    Serum Electrolytes: Decreased sodium (Na) and potassium (K) levels.
  • Nasogastric (N/G) suction
    For differential diagnosis, it relieves pain in hours for simple obstruction but not for strangulated obstruction; it relieves distention in paralytic ileus.
  • Double enema test

ü¶† Malignant Diseases of Intestine

Peutz-Jegher's Syndrome

  • Diagnosed based on multiple familial intestinal hamartomatous polyps, mainly affecting the jejunum, and melanosis of the oral mucous membrane and lips. No specific investigations are listed for diagnosis beyond clinical features.

Familial Polyposis Coli (FPC) and Gardner's Syndrome

  • Barium enema
    Shows multiple rounded filling defects throughout the colon and rectum.
  • Sigmoidectomy or colonoscopy and biopsy
    To prove the nature of the disease.
  • Genetic screening
    Performed for family members.

Carcinoma of the Colon and Rectum

  • Blood picture
    May reveal microcytic hypochromic anemia.
  • Sigmoidoscopy
    Essential for all patients with altered bowel habits or rectal bleeding, mandatory for patients over 40 complaining of piles. Biopsy obtained from suspicious lesions.
  • Colonoscopy
    Biopsy from higher tumors can be obtained.
  • Barium enema
    Shows the tumor as a fixed irregular filling defect. Annular strictures of the left colon show a characteristic "apple core appearance." Useful to exclude a second higher tumor even with a palpable rectal cancer.
  • Liver function tests, abdominal ultrasound (or CT scan), and chest X-ray
    To detect spread.
  • Intravenous urogram (IVU)
    Recommended if the tumor is expected to be close to the ureter.
  • Transrectal ultrasonography or rectal protocol magnetic resonance imaging (MRI)
    Integral for staging rectal tumors to evaluate depth of invasion, circumferential resection margin (CRM), and lymph node status.
  • Carcinoembryonic antigen (CEA)
    A tumor marker, high in colorectal cancer but not specific. Useful for prognosis and follow-up after surgery.
  • For acute intestinal obstruction
    Plain X-ray of the abdomen, blood picture, blood urea, and electrolytes are needed. Barium enema can confirm diagnosis.

üü° Malignant Obstructive Jaundice

Cholangiocarcinoma

  • Laboratory Studies
    Suggest biliary obstruction with elevations in total and direct bilirubin, Alkaline phosphatase (usually increased two- to 10-fold), 5'-nucleotidase, and gamma-glutamyltransferase.
  • Tumor Markers
    CEA and CA 19-9 may be present, though their diagnostic utility is limited. Biliary insulin-like growth factor I is potentially useful.
  • Radiographic Evaluation
    Right upper quadrant ultrasound
    Dynamic CT scan
    EUS (Endoscopic Ultrasound)
    Intraductal US
    ERCP (Endoscopic Retrograde Cholangiopancreatography) with brushing/biopsy
    MRCP (Magnetic Resonance Cholangiopancreatography): Emerging as the imaging technique of choice for hilar lesions
    EUS with fine needle aspiration (FNA): Higher sensitivity than ERCP with brushings and biopsies
    PET scans: Can detect nodular cholangiocarcinomas as small as 1 cm and identify occult metastases

Gallbladder Cancer

  • Laboratory findings
    Not diagnostic but may show abnormalities due to biliary obstruction.
  • Ultrasonography
    Often reveals a thickened, irregular gallbladder wall, mass replacing the gallbladder, or diffuse wall calcification. Can visualize tumor invasion of the liver, lymphadenopathy, and a dilated biliary tree.
  • CT scan
    Important for staging and may identify a gallbladder mass or local invasion into adjacent organs.

Exocrine Pancreatic Cancer

  • Symptoms
    Gradual onset, non-specific (dull epigastric pain, malaise, nausea, fatigue, weight loss), "painless jaundice" (classic, but can be painful), pruritus, darkening of urine, light-colored stools, new-onset diabetes.
  • Examination
    Jaundice, weight loss, palpable abdominal mass (Courvoisier sign), Trousseau's sign (migratory thrombophlebitis), ascites, splenomegaly.
  • Laboratory Tests
    None singularly diagnostic. Elevated serum bilirubin, elevated alkaline phosphatase, mild elevations of serum glutamic-oxaloacetic transaminase and serum glutamate pyruvate transaminase.
  • Tumor Markers
    CA19-9 (useful for confirmation and follow-up, not screening), Carcinoembryonic antigen (CEA) (elevated in 40-50% of patients).
  • Radiologic Studies
    CT scan: Appears as a hypoattenuating indistinct mass, often with dilated pancreatic or biliary ductal system ("double-duct" sign). Criteria for resectability include absence of distant metastasis, preserved fat plane between mass and SMV/PV, absence of tumor encasement of hepatic, celiac, or superior mesenteric arteries.
    Percutaneous (CT- or ultrasonography guided) needle biopsies: Limited role; negative biopsy does not rule out carcinoma.
    Positron emission tomography (PET): Limited to occult metastatic disease.
    Ultrasonography: Not as sensitive as CT, limited by bowel gas.
    MRI: May provide as much information as CT.
    Endoscopic ultrasonography (EUS): Assesses tumor size, portal and mesenteric vascular involvement, and obtains tissue samples.
    Staging laparoscopy: Useful for identifying small liver metastases and peritoneal implants.

Endocrine Pancreatic Neoplasms

  • Diagnosis
    Recognition of classic clinical syndrome; confirmation by measuring serum levels of elevated hormone.
  • Radiological Diagnosis
    Multidetector CT scan with four phases of contrast and fine cuts through the pancreas and liver.
    EUS: Valuable in localizing tumors.
    Angiography with venous sampling: Rarely required for accurate localization.

Insulinoma

  • Clinical Picture
    Whipple's Triad (symptoms of hypoglycemia, blood glucose < 45 mg/dl during attack, improvement with glucose).
  • Investigations
    Decreased Serum glucose, increased insulin, insulin/glucose ratio > 0.3, C-peptides.
  • Localization
    CT, selective angiography, and selective venous sampling.

Gastrinoma (Zollinger Ellison Syndrome)

  • Barium meal
    Shows multiple ulcers in unusual sites.
  • Endoscopy
    Shows multiple ulcers in unusual sites.
  • Gastrin Level
    By radioimmunoassay; normal < 150 ng/L, most patients > 500 ng/L.
  • Localization
    CT scan, selective arteriography, selective venous sampling, EUS.

VIPoma (Vasoactive Intestinal Peptide-Secreting Tumor)

  • Electrolyte abnormalities
    Should raise suspicion.
  • Serum VIP levels
    Must be measured on multiple occasions.
  • CT scan
    Localizes most VIPomas.
  • EUS
    Most sensitive imaging method.

Glucagonoma

  • Diagnosis
    Confirmed by measuring serum glucagon levels (usually >500 pg/mL).

Somatostatinoma

  • Diagnosis
    Confirmed by elevated serum somatostatin levels (usually >10 ng/mL).

Cystic Pancreatic Neoplasms (CPNs)

  • Fluid Analysis
    Carcinoembryonic antigen (CEA): High in Mucinous Cystic Neoplasms (MCNs) and Intraductal Papillary Mucinous Tumors (IPMTs); low in serous cysts and pseudocysts.
    CA 19-9: Can be elevated in malignant cyst neoplasms.
    CA 72-4: Low in pseudocysts and serous tumors; elevated in benign mucinous cystadenomas and very high in cystic malignancy.
    Amylase and lipase: Generally high in pseudocysts and low in cystic tumors.
    Fluid viscosity: Elevated in mucinous tumors and low in pseudocysts and serous cystic tumors.
  • Percutaneous cytology
    Limited value for detecting malignant cells.
  • Percutaneous wall biopsy
    Differentiates between pseudocysts and cystic neoplasms, and between serous and mucinous types.

üö® Acute Abdomen

General Investigations

  • Laboratory
    Full blood picture
    Urea and electrolytes
    Blood sugar
    Serum amylase
    Urine analysis
  • Radiological
    Plain X-ray of the chest: Erect position may show free gas under the diaphragm in viscus perforation; detects basal pneumonia.
    Plain X-ray of the abdomen: Shows calculi of the urinary tract, distended bowel loops, single or multiple fluid levels in intestinal obstruction.
    Abdominal ultrasound: Detects acute calcular cholecystitis (stones, distended GB, thickened walls, subserosal oedema, pericholecystic fluid), acute pancreatitis (enlargement, pseudocyst/abscess, necrosis, fluid collection), leaking aortic aneurysm, distended pelvicalyceal system, gynecological disorders (ruptured/twisted ovarian cyst, ectopic pregnancy, PID).
    CT scan of the abdomen: Not affected by obesity or ileus. With oral and IV contrast, useful for acute pancreatitis (extent of enlargement, gas, fluid, blood, necrosis), retroperitoneal hemorrhage, bowel infarction (gas in intramural/mesenteric veins/portal vein, bowel wall thickening, mesenteric thrombus), splenic infarction, diverticulitis (bowel wall thickness, abscess), localized fluid collection/free fluid, intestinal obstruction (site of transition, etiology).
  • Diagnostic Laparoscopy
    Valuable, especially for gynecological problems.

Acute Appendicitis

  • Leucocytic count
    Polymorphnuclear leucocytosis and shift to the left; absence does not rule out.
  • Urine examination, plain X-ray UT, IVU, ultrasound
    If ureteric calculus suspected.
  • Pelvic ultrasound and laparoscopy
    If tubo-ovarian or uterine problem suspected.
  • Ultrasound
    Appendix >6mm diameter, thickened wall, periappendiceal fluid, not compressible, faecolith. Useful to exclude other mimicking diseases.
  • CT scanning
    Dilated appendix >6mm, thickened wall, thick mesoappendix; identifies other inflammatory processes.
  • Laparoscopy
    Exploratory potential, can diagnose and treat.
  • Pregnancy test
    If ectopic pregnancy suspected.

Acute Cholecystitis

  • Ultrasound
    Diagnostic; detects gallstones (95%), distension of GB, thickened walls, pericholecystic fluid collection, localized tenderness.

Acute Pancreatitis

  • Serum amylase
    Rises within 12 hours, normal in 2-3 days; no predictive value.
  • Plain X-ray of the abdomen
    Excludes other problems.
  • Ultrasound
    Reveals enlargement of pancreas, peripancreatic fluid collection, or pancreatic necrosis.

Acute Diverticulitis

  • Gastrografin enema
    May be helpful.
  • Barium enema
    Should be postponed.
  • CT
    May reveal localized thickening of colonic wall, density in pericolic fat, or pericolic abscess.

Perforated Peptic Ulcer

  • Plain X-ray of the chest (erect position)
    Reveals free gas under the diaphragm in 60-85% of cases.
  • Gastrografin meal
    Reveals leakage if X-ray does not show free gas.

Simple Bowel Obstruction

  • Plain X-ray
    Reveals distended loops or fluid levels.
  • CT scan with oral contrast
    Very useful in doubtful cases.
  • Barium enema
    Diagnostic in large bowel obstruction.

Strangulation Intestinal Obstruction

  • Leucocytosis

Mesenteric Ischaemia

  • Plain X-ray
    May reveal ground glass appearance.
  • Ultrasound and CT scans
    May show occluding thrombus, bowel wall oedema or abnormal gas patterns.
  • CT angiography
    Shows occlusion of superior mesenteric artery orifice or embolus.

Ruptured Ovarian Cyst

  • Abdominal and pelvic ultrasound examinations
    Diagnostic.

Torsion of an Ovarian Cyst

  • Ultrasound
    Diagnostic.
  • Laparoscopy
    Diagnostic and therapeutic.

Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID)

  • High vaginal swab
    For culture and sensitivity.
  • Ultrasound

Ruptured Ectopic Pregnancy

  • Chorionic gonadotropin testing
    Positive.
  • Ultrasound
    Presence of gestational sac in uterus is against diagnosis; free blood in pelvis.
  • Laparoscopy
    Very helpful.

ü´Ä Liver Diseases

Hepatic Cysts

  • Imaging
    Often identified incidentally on imaging for other symptoms.
  • Distinction from simple cysts
    Presence of a mass or septa distinguishes neoplastic cystic lesions.

Hemangioma

  • Laboratory
    Not helpful.
  • Needle biopsy
    Not done.
  • Ultrasound
    Well-defined, lobulated, homogeneous, hyperechoic mass (sometimes hypoechoic in fatty livers or with atypical features).
  • Multiphasic (contrast) CT scans
    Low-density area with characteristic peripheral enhancement in early phase, progressing centrally, uniformly enhanced in delayed images.
  • MRI
    Best imaging study; smooth, lobulated, homogeneous, hypointense on T1-weighted; hyperintense on T2-weighted.
  • Single photon emission CT (SPECT) with technetium-labeled red blood cells
    Similar accuracy to MRI for lesions >3 cm near surface.

Focal Nodular Hyperplasia (FNH)

  • Ultrasound
    First detects focal hepatic lesions but cannot differentiate FNH from other pathology.
  • Multiphasic CT
    Bright homogeneous enhancement with a hypodense central scar in late arterial phase; central scar and radiating septa may hyperattenuate in delayed phase.
  • MRI
    Central scar appears hyperintense on T2-weighted images; similar enhancement pattern to CT with contrast.

Hepatic Adenoma (HA)

  • Ultrasound
    Variable echogenicity; often heterogeneous due to hemorrhage/necrosis. Color Doppler may show peripheral peritumoral and intratumoral vessels.
  • CT
    Frequently appears heterogeneous due to intratumor hemorrhage, necrosis. Arterial-phase CT shows multiple hypervascular lesions; portal venous-phase shows isoattenuating relative to surrounding parenchyma.

Hepatocellular Carcinoma (HCC)

  • Laboratory
    Abnormal liver function tests; elevated serum Œ±-fetoprotein (AFP) (>200 ng/mL suggestive).
  • Radiologic studies
    Ultrasound: Highly accurate, especially with AFP elevations; hyperechoic, smaller tumors hypoechoic.
    Multiphasic CT scan: Enhances in arterial phase, washout of contrast in delayed phase.
    MRI: Most accurate for distinguishing HCC from dysplastic or regenerative nodules; variable intensity on T1, almost always hyperintense on T2; tumor capsule hypointense on T1 and T2.
    Laparoscopic or image-guided biopsies.

Pyogenic Liver Abscess

  • Laboratory findings
    Nonspecific; leukocytosis, elevated alkaline phosphatase, ESR.
  • Radiological
    Chest x-ray (elevation of right hemidiaphragm, infiltrate, pleural effusion); CT scanning for definitive diagnosis.

Amebic Abscess

  • Diagnosis
    Serologic tests for amebic infestation (nearly 100% positive); leukocytosis, stool analysis.
  • Imaging
    Ultrasound and CT are most useful.

Echinococcal Cysts (Hydatid Disease)

  • Imaging
    Nuclear medicine scan, ultrasound, CT scan, or MRI can detect the lesion.
  • Laboratory
    Peripheral eosinophilia often detected. Serologic tests include indirect hemagglutinin and Casoni skin test. Cyst not aspirated for diagnosis.

ü´ò Spleen & Splenomegaly

Rupture of the Spleen

  • Repeated blood picture
    Declining haemoglobin and haematocrit denote haemorrhage.
  • Ultrasound or CT scan of the abdomen
    Diagnostic accuracy >90%; monitor hematoma size.
  • Plain x-ray of the abdomen
    Poor diagnostic substitute but may reveal fractured lower ribs.
  • Peritoneal lavage
    Reveals blood.

Splenomegaly

  • History and examination findings
  • Laboratory examination
  • Haemolytic anaemia
    CBC, reticulocyte count, tests for haemolysis.
  • Portal hypertension
    Liver function tests (abnormal), CBC (anaemia, leukopenia, thrombocytopenia), endoscopic evidence of oesophageal varices.
  • Radiological Imaging
    Plain radiology: Rarely used, may show calcification of splenic artery/spleen (aneurysm, old infarct, benign cyst, hydatid disease), multiple areas of calcification may suggest TB.
    Ultrasonography: Determine size and consistency, presence of cyst.
    Computed tomography (CT) with contrast enhancement: Better characterizes splenic pathology and excludes other intra-abdominal pathology.
    Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI): Similarly useful.
    Radioisotope scanning: Occasionally used (technetium 99m).

Hypersplenism

  • Investigations
    Blood picture: Reveals low counts of RBCs, leucocytes, and platelets; elevated reticulocyte count.
    Peripheral blood smear: Devoid of diagnostic features of leukaemia or myeloproliferative disease.
    Bone marrow: Reveals pancellular hyperplasia.
    Radioactive chromium (51Cr) tagged RBCs: Confirms diminished half-life of RBCs and elevated spleen/liver radioactivity ratio.

Hyposplenism

  • Diagnosis
    Determined by anatomic presence or absence of the organ, its size, and any lesions.
  • Function Assessment
    Radiologic techniques: X-ray, ultrasound, tomography, MRI.
    Morphologically: Peripheral blood smear, presence of Howell-Jolly bodies.

Infections of the Spleen

  • Acute Abscess
    Percutaneous drainage under ultrasound or CT guidance.
  • Hydatid Disease
    Imaging by nuclear medicine scan, ultrasound, CT scan, or MRI. Peripheral eosinophilia often detected. Serologic tests (indirect hemagglutinin, Casoni skin test). Cyst not aspirated for diagnosis.

GIST (Gastro-intestinal Stromal Tumor)

  • Gastrografin meal

  • CT scan abdomen

Carcinoma of The Stomach

  • EGD
    Diagnostic + biopsy.
  • Gastrografin meal
    Persistent filling defect, ulcer, severe narrowing (linitis plastica).
  • CT abdomen triphasic or spiral

  • CEA level

    For distant metastasis.
  • Diagnostic laparoscopy

ü´Å Surgical Disease of Peritoneum & Mesentery

Acute Peritonitis

  • Laboratory
    CBC (anemia), KFTs (pre-renal failure), Serum electrolytes.
  • Radiological
    Chest x-rays: Differential diagnosis (pneumonia).
    Plain abdominal x-rays: Supine and upright positions; gas beneath the diaphragm indicates GI perforation.
    Gastrografin: Passed into stomach via nasogastric tube to demonstrate perforation (does not irritate peritoneum as barium).
    Ultrasound: Helps in differential diagnosis (gallstone, GB wall thickness, fluid collection, tumors, kidney stone).
    CT SCAN: For intraabdominal abscess.
  • Laparoscopy
    Most important diagnostic and therapeutic measure.

Subphrenic Abscess

  • Investigations
    Routine: CBC (leucocytosis), CXR (elevated copula of diaphragm, pleural effusion, localized gas).
    Diagnosis: Abdominal U/S & CT to localize abscess.

Pelvic Abscess

  • Physical examination (PR or PV)
    Reveals tense, cystic and tender mass.

Iliac Abscess

  • Investigations
    CBC (‚Üë TLC), U/S.

Mesenteric Cysts

  • U/S- CT scan
  • IVP
    (excludes renal cyst).
  • Plain X-Ray
    Calcified hydatid or mesenteric LNs.

Peritoneal Tumors

  • Peritoneal carcinomatosis
    Diagnosed by gross picture of peritoneum studded with malignant nodules and ascitic fluid. No specific investigations listed beyond clinical findings.
  • Pseudomyxoma peritonei
    Abdominal examination shows distended abdomen without shifting dullness, multiple firm masses palpable. No specific investigations listed.
  • Mesothelioma
    Suspected by exposure to asbestos. No specific investigations listed.

Retroperitoneal Tumors

  • Retroperitoneal sarcoma
    Ultrasound and CT scan are very useful for diagnosis; CT accurately localizes the lesion, its size, and reveals infiltration of adjacent structures.

Torsion of the Omentum

  • No specific investigations listed, primarily clinical picture.

üîÄ Surgical Disorders of Esophagus

Esophageal Perforations

  • Inv
    Chest x-ray, Gastrografin swallow (leakage site).

Corrosive Esophageal Injury

  • Inv
    Chest and Abdomen x-ray (detect perforation), Endoscopy (after 24-48 hrs), Gastrografin swallow (after 3 wks).

Achalasia of Oesophagus

  • Inv
    Gastrografin swallow: Shows hold of dye at cardia, proximal dilatation, tortuosity (sigmoid oesophagus), smooth termination ("hen's beak").
    Oesophagoscopy: Shows dilatation, retained food, failure of LES to relax, excludes carcinoma.
    Manometry: Weak peristalsis, high pressure LES.

Hiatus Hernia (Sliding HH)

  • Investigations
    EGD.
    Gastrografin meal in Trendelenburg position: Reflux of dye, appearance of bulge.
    Oesophageal manometry: Low pressure at LES.
    PH metry over 24 hrs: Probe positioned 5 cm above LES, patient records PH during symptoms of reflux (low PH indicates acid reflux).

Hiatus Hernia (Paraesophageal HH)

  • Inv.
    CXR (gastric fundus air in chest), GG meal, CT chest and upper abdomen.

Cancer Oesophagus

  • Inv
    Gastrografin swallow: Irregular filling defect, irregular narrowing + shouldering.
    Oesophagoscopy: To diagnose + biopsy.
    CT chest and abdomen: To detect size, liver metastasis, pulmonary nodules, infiltration to adjacent structures, LNs.
    Endoluminal US: To detect mediastinum infiltration.
    PET (Positron Emission Tomography) CT: To detect distant metastasis.
    Bronchoscopy: To detect tracheal and bronchial involvement.

üìç The Appendix to the Caecum

Acute Appendicitis

  • Leucocyte count
    Moderate leucocytosis (10,000-16,000/uL) with neutrophil predominance in 80-90% of cases; normal count does not rule out.
  • Urine analysis
    May show few red blood cells; large number of RBCs or pus cells raises suspicion of ureteric calculus or UTI.
  • Pregnancy test
    If ectopic pregnancy suspected.
  • Ultrasound examination
    Appendix >6mm diameter, thickened wall, periappendiceal fluid, not compressible; faecolith detected. Useful for excluding gynecological problems (transvaginal US) and right ureter stones (back pressure changes in kidney).
  • CT scanning
    Demonstrates dilated appendix (>6mm), thickened wall, thick mesoappendix; identifies other inflammatory processes.
  • Laparoscopy
    Policy in some centers for females with right lower abdominal pain; may reveal non-surgical problem or allow surgical management.

Appendicular Abscess

  • Ultrasound examination
    Confirms presence of fluid.

  • Tc Scan + gamma camera

TB of the Intestine

Surgical Complications of Typhoid and Paratyphoid

  • Primarily based on clinical picture; no specific investigations listed for diagnosis of these complications, though peritonitis and hemorrhage would have general acute abdomen investigations.

Small Intestinal Fistula

  • Barium meal follow through
  • Barium enema
  • Fistulogram

Intestinal Trauma

  • Diagnosis
    Mainly based on clinical findings.
  • Laboratory
    Leucocytosis and hemodilution.
  • Radiological
    Plain x-ray of the abdomen: May show free air under the diaphragm, multiple fluid levels due to ileus, fractures.
    U/S or CT scan: May show hematoma or intraperitoneal collection.
  • DPL (Diagnostic Peritoneal Lavage)

Ulcerative Colitis

  • Radiological (Barium enema)
    Colonic shortening, loss of haustrations ("pipe-stem appearance"), granular mucosa, pseudopolyps.
  • Instrumental (Colonoscopy and biopsy)
    Best method; shows mucosal abnormalities.
  • Laboratory
    CBC (anemia, leucocytosis in acute cases), Hypoproteinemia and hypokalemia (in severe cases).

Crohn's Disease (Regional Ileitis)

  • Radiological (Barium meal follow through)
    Early shows cobblestone appearances due to longitudinal fissures, skip lesions; late shows String sign of Kantor (marked narrowing).
  • Instrumental (Colonoscopy and biopsy)
    Proves diagnosis of large bowel lesions; biopsy of suspected anal fissure or fistula.
  • Laboratory
    CBC (Anemia), ESR & CRP (both elevated), Biochemical profile and serum proteins (low serum albumin, iron, folate; ‚Üì Ca, Mg, Zn, selenium, Vit A in severe disease).

üü¢ Diseases of Biliary System

Gall Bladder Stones (Symptomatic)

  • Abdominal U/S (investigation of choice)
    Detects size & thickness of GB & presence of stone (98% of cases); detects CBD diameter and intrahepatic biliary dilatation. Requires fasting. Shows echoes, acoustic shadows, and dependent positions.
  • CT/MRI
  • Plain X-ray
    Only 10-15% are radio-opaque; multiple, faceted signet ring.

Acute Calculous Cholecystitis

  • Lab
    TLC > 12000, Elevated Bilirubin, Alk. Phosph.
  • Imaging U/S
    Gallstone, thick wall, pericholecystic collection, tenderness on probing, empyema.
  • CT/MRI

Jaundice (Obstructive Jaundice)

  • Laboratory
    LFTs (Bilirubin, Alk. Phosphatase, Gamma Gt, ALT and AST), BUN and Creatinine, CBC, Stool, Urine.
  • Radiological
    Abdominal U/S, MRCP, ERCP, PTC.

Choledochal Cyst

  • Radiology
    No specific ones listed, but MRCP/ERCP would be implied by the context of biliary tree imaging.

Gallstone Ileus

  • X-ray and CT
    Shows Rigler's triad (Intestinal obstruction, pneumobilia, aberrant gallstone in intestine).

Mirizzi Syndrome

  • Diagnosis
    Most sensitive is ERCP.

Carcinoma of Gall Bladder

  • Investigations
    US, CT, MRI.

Strictures of Bile Duct (Neoplastic Stricture/Cholangiocarcinoma)

  • C/P
    Obstructive jaundice (90%), pain, cholangitis, anorexia.
  • Treatment
    ERCP with metallic stent or cholecystojejunostomy for inoperable cases.
