Described as an "obstructive jaundice" type of cancer, meaning it causes jaundice by blocking the flow of bile. It is a malignancy originating in the bile ducts.

Epidemiology

Incidence

Relatively uncommon GI tract cancer: ~2 cases per 100,000 people

Age Distribution

Affects older age groups more frequently than younger ones

Problem with Diagnosis

Significant problem is delayed diagnosis. Patients often present at advanced stage when curative resection is difficult or impossible, leading to poor prognosis.

Pathology

Most common histological type: Adenocarcinoma (90%), originating from the mucosal lining of the bile ducts

Classification (Location)

Peripheral (Intrahepatic)

10-20% of cases

Occurs within the liver parenchyma. Often presents similarly to Hepatocellular Carcinoma (HCC)

Central (Perihilar/Hilar)

40-60% of cases

Most common type. Occurs at confluence of right and left hepatic ducts. Surgically challenging due to porta hepatis location

Distal

20-30% of cases

Occurs in common bile duct (CBD) down to ampulla of Vater. 10-15% of periampullary carcinomas

Most common presentation, typically gradual in onset

Can occur even before overt jaundice is noticeable. Sometimes leads to misdiagnosis as dermatological issue

Other Symptoms (30-50%)

Fever, abdominal pain, weight loss, cachexia (less common, usually in advanced stages)

Cholangitis

Obstruction and stasis can lead to bacterial infection

Diagnosis

Laboratory Tests

  • Elevated Bilirubin: Primarily direct (conjugated) bilirubin in obstructive jaundice
  • Elevated ALP: Can be elevated from bone or bile ducts
  • Elevated GGT: More specific for biliary obstruction when elevated with ALP
  • CA 19-9: Tumor marker for biliary system tumors (not highly specific)

Imaging

Ultrasound

Initial imaging. Identifies dilated bile ducts, sometimes visualizes mass/stones

CT Scan

Staging, assessing mass extent, looking for metastasis

MRCP

Highly valuable, detailed biliary tree visualization

ERCP

Direct visualization, therapeutic interventions, cytology/biopsy

EUS

Best for assessing resectability, vascular invasion, guided FNA

PET Scan

Assessing distant metastasis

Staging

Bismuth-Corlette Classification

Specifically for perihilar cholangiocarcinoma. Classifies tumor based on extension within biliary tree. Important for surgical planning and resectability assessment.

AJCC TNM Staging

Describes primary tumor (T), regional lymph nodes (N), and distant metastasis (M).

‚úÇÔ∏è Treatment

Surgery (Resection) - Only Potential for Cure

Resectability depends heavily on location, extent of tumor, and involvement of major vessels.

Periampullary/Distal Lesions

Often treated with Whipple procedure (Pancreaticoduodenectomy) as lesions often involve head of pancreas and duodenum

Perihilar/Hilar Lesions

Surgically challenging. May require complex resections involving liver parenchyma (hepatectomy) and challenging biliary reconstructions (Hepaticojejunostomy, Roux-en-Y)

Intrahepatic Lesions

Treated with liver resection, similar to HCC treatment approach

Liver Transplantation

Potential option but with significant debate and challenges due to high risk of recurrence compared to HCC. Specific protocols may involve neoadjuvant chemotherapy and radiation.

Biliary Drainage

Essential for managing obstructive jaundice, especially in unresectable cases or as bridge to surgery

Internal Drainage (Preferred)

Via ERCP with stent placement

Bile drains into GI tract, aiding nutrient absorption and preventing external fluid loss

External Drainage

Via PTC and PTD catheter placement

Bile drains into external bag

Other Therapies

Chemotherapy and Radiotherapy

  • ‚Ä¢ Adjuvant (after surgery)
  • ‚Ä¢ Neoadjuvant (before surgery)
  • ‚Ä¢ Palliative settings

Photodynamic Therapy (PDT)

Palliative option to relieve obstruction by destroying tumor cells with light after administering photosensitizing agent

Resectability Criteria

Patient Factors

Overall health, fitness for surgery (ASA score), liver function

Local Tumor Extent

Involvement of major blood vessels (portal vein, hepatic artery). Encasement makes tumor unresectable

Distant Metastasis

Presence makes tumor unresectable for cure. PET scan helpful for detection

Biliary System Involvement

Involvement of both right and left biliary systems (Bismuth-Corlette Type IV)

üî¨ II. Gallbladder Cancer

Definition & Epidemiology

Definition: Cancer of the gallbladder, described as the 6th most common GI cancer

Gender Distribution: More common in females than males (3:1 ratio)

Clinical Presentation

‚Ä¢ Can be asymptomatic

‚Ä¢ Symptoms similar to gallstones (biliary colic)

‚Ä¢ Advanced cancer: jaundice, palpable mass

‚Ä¢ Often incidental finding during cholecystectomy (1-5% of cases)

Risk Factors

Gallstones

Most common risk factor

Larger stones (>1.5 cm, some say >1 cm or >2.5 cm) associated with higher malignancy risk

Porcelain Gallbladder

Calcification of gallbladder wall - strong risk factor

Gallbladder Polyps

Risk increases with size. Polyps >1 cm generally recommended for cholecystectomy

Chronic Inflammation

Similar to cholangiocarcinoma (e.g., Salmonella typhi carriers)

Anomalous Junction

Pancreaticobiliary junction where pancreatic duct joins CBD outside sphincter of Oddi

Gallbladder Polyp Malignancy Risk

Polyp Size Malignancy Risk Recommendation
<1 cm Low risk (5%) Follow-up
1-1.5 cm Higher risk (10-12%) Consider surgery
>1.5 cm Significantly higher risk (up to 40%) Cholecystectomy recommended

Treatment

Cholecystectomy

Standard treatment for early-stage, incidentally found cancers or high-risk polyps

Extended Cholecystectomy

For advanced stages: gallbladder + surrounding liver tissue (segments IVb and V) + regional lymph nodes

Role of Laparoscopy

While laparoscopic cholecystectomy is standard for benign disease, it may be contraindicated for suspected/diagnosed gallbladder cancer due to risk of port site metastasis or inadequate resection. Open surgery often preferred.

Staging & Prognosis

CT scan essential for staging, assessing local liver invasion and porta hepatis involvement. Generally poor prognosis, especially for advanced stages.
